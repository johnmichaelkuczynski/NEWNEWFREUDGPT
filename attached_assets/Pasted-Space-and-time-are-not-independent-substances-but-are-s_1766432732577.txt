Space and time are not independent substances but are sets of coordinates that provide the framework for individuating objects and events, a position necessitated by the analysis of representational content and truth-makers. This view emerges from the structural analysis of perception and thought, where the veridicality of a mental state is determined by specific configurations within regions of space-time. As established in the analysis of content, "When I look at an ice-cube, what makes my perception veridical is that there are various H2O-molecules in a certain configuration in a certain region of space-time." The truth-maker is thus a proposition about objects and properties instantiated within a designated region R. Space and time are the ordering systems that allow us to identify region R as distinct from region R*, which is crucial for externalist semantics. They are not themselves occupants with causal powers but are the abstract, coordinate-based structures that make individuation possible. This is clarified by the point that "spatiotemporal points and lines, and so forth, lack causal powers. But they are not occupants of space-time. Rather, they are sets of space-time coordinates." Therefore, space and time constitute the formal, relational system that permits the identification of the particular referents of our thoughts.

The nature of space and time is further illuminated by the asymmetry between representation and projection in cognitive architecture. A subject can represent a region R without thereby projecting any properties onto it; the representational content is fixed by causal connections to external particulars. Consider the case of Max and Twin-Max. "Max’s visual perception is veridical exactly if R has certain properties, it being irrelevant whether R* has those properties. And Twin-Max’s visual perception is veridical exactly if R* has certain properties, it being irrelevant whether R has those properties." This thought experiment demonstrates that the identity of a thought's object is determined by its causal linkage to a specific region within the space-time matrix. Space and time provide the scaffold for this causal-computational convergence, enabling a brain state to be a belief about a particular rock in region R rather than its duplicate in region R*. Without this coordinate system, conceptual atomism—the thesis that a concept can consist solely in a causal connection to an object—would be unintelligible, as there would be no framework to individuate the object of that causal connection.

Consequently, space and time are necessary conditions for possessing concepts of particulars, refuting a radical conceptual atomism that would allow an otherwise conceptless being to have a concept. The texts argue that "Supposing that X is a creature that has no conception of space or time or corporeality, could X have a concept of Socrates? Most of us would say 'no.'" This reveals that space and time are not merely external coordinates but are also represented within the conceptual network; a minimal grasp of them is required for having any concepts of concrete entities. They are the forms of individuation, both in the world and in thought. The analysis of truth-makers shows that what a perception tells me is not the complex molecular proposition but something more coarse-grained, like 'there is an ice cube there.' The 'there' implicitly references a location within the spatiotemporal system. Thus, space and time are the indispensable frameworks for both the metaphysics of particulars and the semantics of singular thought, serving as the interface between causal relations and representational content.

In conclusion, space and time are the coordinate systems that individuate regions and particulars, making them the formal grounds for truth-makers and the objects of thought. They lack causal efficacy themselves but are prerequisites for the causal connections that underpin conceptual content. This view, derived from the rigorous analysis of perception, externalism, and conceptual atomism, positions space and time not as mysterious substances but as the structural axes of reality that enable representation and cognition. Any adequate philosophy of mind and language must treat them as the fundamental scaffolds for identifying what our thoughts are about, without which the very distinction between Max's rock and Twin-Max's rock collapses. Their necessity in the conceptual architecture confirms that while concepts may be atomistic in their externalist grounding, they are not atomistic in their cognitive prerequisites, requiring at least a minimal grasp of spatiotemporal framework.
