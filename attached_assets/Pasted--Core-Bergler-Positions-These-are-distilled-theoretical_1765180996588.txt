# Core Bergler Positions

These are distilled theoretical propositions that underlie his clinical and theoretical work. They're organized thematically.

---

## I. THE BASIC NEUROSIS AND ORAL REGRESSION

1. All neuroses ultimately derive from disturbances in the earliest oral phase of development—the infant's relationship with the mother's breast.

2. The infant experiences the breast as omnipotent and itself as the controller of that omnipotence; inevitable frustration shatters this illusion.

3. This frustration produces "oral pessimism"—a fundamental conviction that the world is denying, refusing, and withholding.

4. The infant responds to oral frustration with aggression toward the mother, but this aggression is immediately turned inward due to helplessness and dependency.

5. This internalized aggression becomes the nucleus of the punishing superego.

6. The "basic neurosis" is the unconscious need to recreate and re-experience early oral disappointment—to prove again and again that the world refuses.

7. All later developmental stages (anal, phallic, genital) are overlays on this oral foundation; apparent Oedipal conflicts often mask deeper oral injuries.

---

## II. PSYCHIC MASOCHISM

8. Psychic masochism is the unconscious seeking of psychic pain, defeat, humiliation, and failure—not for pleasure in pain itself, but as a solution to deeper conflicts.

9. Psychic masochism is distinct from conscious suffering; the masochist consciously wants success and happiness but unconsciously arranges failure and misery.

10. The masochist does not enjoy suffering—he suffers from suffering but cannot stop arranging it.

11. Masochism represents a "lesser evil" solution: by choosing one's own suffering, one maintains an illusion of control and avoids the greater terror of unpredictable external harm.

12. Masochistic suffering also appeases the superego's demand for punishment, temporarily reducing unconscious guilt.

13. Everyone has masochistic tendencies; the question is only of degree and the specific form they take.

14. Masochism expresses itself in all domains: love, work, creativity, money, health, and social relations.

---

## III. THE MECHANISM OF NEUROSIS: THE TRIAD

15. Neurotic symptoms and character traits operate through a characteristic triad of unconscious mechanisms.

16. First mechanism: The unconscious ego creates or provokes a situation of refusal, rejection, or mistreatment—recreating the original oral disappointment.

17. Second mechanism: The ego responds to this (self-created) disappointment with aggression and indignation, experiencing itself as the innocent victim of injustice.

18. Third mechanism: This aggression, being directed at an internal maternal image, triggers guilt and the need for punishment, which the symptom or character pattern itself provides.

19. The neurotic is thus simultaneously the criminal, the victim, and the executioner—all roles are played unconsciously.

20. The conscious experience is only of victimization; the unconscious authorship of one's own suffering remains hidden.

---

## IV. THE SUPEREGO

21. The superego forms primarily from internalized maternal prohibitions and the infant's own aggression turned inward.

22. The superego is not a copy of external authorities but a distorted, magnified, sadistic caricature of them.

23. The superego's cruelty exceeds any actual parental cruelty because it incorporates the child's own projected aggression.

24. The superego demands punishment for unconscious wishes, not for actions—one is guilty for what one unconsciously desires.

25. Unconscious guilt is the driving force behind most self-destructive behavior.

26. The superego uses internal guilt not to reform behavior but to extract suffering as tribute.

27. Different neurotic configurations represent different compromises with superego demands—different ways of paying the "tax" of suffering.

28. The "corrupt superego" accepts disguised payments: symptom-suffering, self-sabotage, and masochistic character patterns all serve as currency.

29. Some superegos can be bribed with suffering in one domain to permit pleasure in another.

---

## V. INJUSTICE COLLECTING

30. The "injustice collector" is a specific character type whose entire psychic economy depends on feeling victimized.

31. The injustice collector unconsciously provokes mistreatment, then genuinely experiences surprise and outrage at being mistreated.

32. The collection of injustices serves multiple purposes: it provides masochistic satisfaction, justifies counter-aggression, and proves the original oral thesis that the world refuses.

33. Injustice collectors are drawn to situations and partners guaranteed to produce grievances.

34. The injustice collector cannot be satisfied by fair treatment—if none is available, he will manufacture mistreatment or interpret neutral events as slights.

35. In marriage, injustice collectors create a self-perpetuating cycle: they provoke, collect the grievance, then use the grievance as justification for further provocation.

---

## VI. WRITER'S BLOCK AND CREATIVITY

36. Writing involves unconscious exhibitionism—the writer displays himself before an internal audience derived from early parental figures.

37. Writer's block occurs when the superego forbids this exhibitionism, or when guilt over unconscious meanings in the work becomes overwhelming.

38. The blocked writer is not lacking ideas but is forbidden by internal authority from expressing them.

39. Many writers have an unconscious equation: writing = forbidden voyeuristic and exhibitionistic gratification.

40. The block often intensifies after success because success increases guilt and provokes superego retaliation.

41. Writer's block frequently represents identification with the "refusing" mother: the writer refuses to "give" his productions just as he felt mother refused to give.

42. The blocked writer often unconsciously identifies writing with prohibited aggressive or sexual content, regardless of the work's actual subject matter.

43. Block can represent a work stoppage in the inner factory—a strike against the internal slave-driving superego.

44. Some blocked writers can only produce under conditions of suffering or deprivation, because suffering bribes the superego into permitting creative expression.

45. Writer's block is curable through analysis because it rests on unconscious conflicts that can be made conscious.

---

## VII. MARRIAGE AND PARTNER SELECTION

46. Partner selection is never accidental; the unconscious chooses partners who will enable the repetition of early neurotic patterns.

47. People unconsciously select partners who will disappoint them in precisely the ways they were originally disappointed.

48. Neurotic partner choice is based not on what the partner actually is but on what the neurotic unconsciously needs the partner to be.

49. The unhappy marriage typically involves two neurotics whose pathologies interlock—each provides the other with the frustration they unconsciously require.

50. Many marriages are unconsciously designed to fail from the beginning; the wedding is already the first step toward divorce.

51. The repetitive divorcer has an unconscious marital pattern that will reproduce itself regardless of the apparent differences between partners.

52. Marital happiness requires that both partners have resolved, to reasonable degree, their oral-masochistic conflicts.

53. Genuine love is based on object-cathexis (investment in the real other), not on narcissistic need (using the other for internal purposes).

54. Sexual adjustment in marriage depends less on technique than on the unconscious meanings sex carries for each partner.

55. Many sexual difficulties in marriage are created by one or both partners for masochistic purposes.

---

## VIII. HOMOSEXUALITY (Historical Position—Now Rejected)

*These represent Bergler's historical views, which are no longer accepted by mainstream psychology and are considered harmful.*

56. Bergler viewed homosexuality as a neurotic symptom rooted in oral regression and masochism.

57. He believed it represented a flight from the opposite sex based on unconscious fears derived from the early maternal relationship.

58. He claimed homosexuality was treatable through psychoanalysis and was not innate.

59. These positions have been repudiated by contemporary psychology, which recognizes homosexuality as a normal variant of human sexuality.

---

## IX. THE FASHION INDUSTRY

60. Fashion exploits universal psychological mechanisms, particularly narcissism, exhibitionism, and masochism.

61. Fashion's constant change reflects an unconscious need for novelty that masks oral dissatisfaction—nothing can satisfy permanently because the original oral wound cannot be healed by external means.

62. Fashion designers often express unconscious aggression toward women through impractical, uncomfortable, or unflattering designs.

63. The fashion victim is a masochistic type who submits to the sadism of arbitrary fashion dictates.

64. Fashion involves identification with the aggressor: by embracing the latest fashion, one joins the powerful group rather than being excluded by it.

65. The willingness to discard last season's clothes enacts the oral drama of things once desired becoming worthless once obtained.

---

## X. SUCCESS, CAREER, AND SELF-SABOTAGE

66. Many professional failures result not from external obstacles but from unconscious self-sabotage.

67. Success can trigger intense unconscious guilt, because success feels like triumphing over parental figures.

68. The "success neurosis" describes those who cannot tolerate success and unconsciously arrange to fail just as they are about to succeed.

69. Some neurotics can only function professionally under conditions of exploitation because they need to feel victimized.

70. Chronic complainers about work conditions often unconsciously select or create the conditions they complain about.

71. The relationship with one's boss often recapitulates the relationship with early authority figures; neurotic work problems frequently reflect unresolved parental conflicts.

72. Talent is no protection against neurotic self-defeat; indeed, talented people often have more elaborate means of sabotaging themselves.

73. Some people can only work when they are "paid" in suffering—deprivation, poor conditions, or exploitation serve as the price that permits productivity.

---

## XI. GAMBLING AND ADDICTION

74. The gambler is not primarily seeking money but is unconsciously staging a drama of loss, proving the thesis that the world refuses.

75. Gambling provides a pure form of masochistic pleasure: the certainty of eventual loss is the unconscious aim, not the conscious hope of winning.

76. The gambler is in rebellion against logic and reality—against the "reality principle" that represents parental authority.

77. The excitement of gambling represents a manic defense against underlying depression.

78. Addiction represents oral regression to a stage where a substance replaces the mother; the addict seeks the illusion of oral fusion.

79. Addictive patterns inevitably become self-punishing, which serves the masochistic component of the underlying neurosis.

80. The addict's relationship to the substance replicates the ambivalent oral relationship: desperate need combined with rage at dependency.

---

## XII. PATHOLOGICAL MASCULINITY

81. Pseudo-aggression and Don Juanism often mask underlying passivity and unconscious feminine identification.

82. The compulsive seducer is not driven by sexual desire but by the need to prove masculinity he unconsciously doubts.

83. The man who needs to dominate women is defending against terror of women derived from the early maternal relationship.

84. Impotence frequently results from unconscious prohibition—the superego forbids sexual success, often due to incestuous meanings.

85. The sadistic man toward women is expressing displaced aggression originally directed at the mother.

86. Men who repeatedly choose "impossible" women are unconsciously arranging for romantic failure.

87. Exaggerated masculinity often indicates fixation at the phallic level covering deeper oral-passive wishes.

---

## XIII. PATHOLOGICAL FEMININITY

88. Frigidity is not a lack of capacity for pleasure but an active inhibition based on unconscious conflict.

89. The frigid woman is typically punishing men (displacing aggression from father), punishing herself (superego tribute), or both.

90. Women who choose unsuitable men are not making errors of judgment but unconscious choices guaranteeing disappointment.

91. Masochistic submission in women often represents aggression turned inward, combined with repetition of early submission to maternal authority.

92. Competition with men sometimes represents identification with the aggressor combined with revenge for early narcissistic injuries.

93. The woman who sabotages her husband's success may be expressing displaced aggression, or may need to maintain his dependency to assuage her own anxieties.

---

## XIV. PSYCHIC STRUCTURE AND DIAGNOSTIC CATEGORIES

94. Neurosis and psychosis differ in degree of ego damage and relationship to reality—neurosis involves symptom formation while maintaining reality testing; psychosis involves ego fragmentation.

95. The specific form of neurosis depends on the developmental point of fixation and the specific defense mechanisms employed.

96. Hysteria primarily employs repression and involves conflicts centered on genital-Oedipal themes, but with underlying oral components.

97. Obsessional neurosis employs isolation, undoing, and reaction formation; it appears anal but often masks oral conflicts.

98. Depression represents aggression turned against the self following real or imagined loss of a love object with whom one is ambivalently identified.

99. Perversions represent fixations at component sexual drives that failed to integrate under genital primacy.

100. Character neurosis is more entrenched than symptom neurosis because the pathology is experienced as part of the self rather than as a foreign symptom.

---

## XV. THE POSSIBILITY OF CURE

101. Neurosis is not destiny; unconscious conflicts made conscious lose their determining power.

102. Analysis works by making the unconscious conscious, thereby expanding the ego's domain at the expense of the id and superego.

103. The patient's resistance to cure is itself a manifestation of the neurosis—part of him clings to his suffering.

104. Genuine cure requires not just intellectual insight but emotional working-through of unconscious conflicts.

105. Many patients seek not cure but more effective neurosis—a version of their pathology that works better.

106. Short-term interventions cannot address character neurosis, which requires extended analytic work.

107. The goal of treatment is not happiness but freedom from neurotic compulsion—what one does with that freedom is one's own affair.

---

These positions constitute the theoretical scaffold for Bergler's clinical work. They're interconnected: the oral origin, masochistic mechanism, superego dynamics, and repetition compulsion together form a unified system for understanding self-defeat in all its manifestations.
