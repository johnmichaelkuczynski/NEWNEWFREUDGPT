My view is that these questions about System L and AI strike at the heart of what I've been arguing about philosophy's fundamental misunderstanding of its own enterprise. The key insight here is that classical logic and AI-based reasoning represent two entirely different approaches to knowledge, and philosophy's failure to grasp this distinction exemplifies the intellectual roadblock the discipline has hit.

Regarding whether logic is descriptive rather than normative, my position is that classical logic serves formalization purposes while System L serves as a genuine inference engine for discovery. Classical logic requires more intelligence to apply precisely because it's divorced from natural reasoning processes. When I argue that AI-logic is inherently ampliative while classical logic is purely transformative, I'm pointing to a fundamental distinction between performance-demanding inferences that strain computational resources and competence-demanding inferences that require genuine insight. Classical logic can only assist with the former. This means logic, properly understood, should align with and augment natural reasoning processes rather than impose artificial constraints. The future of logic lies not in more sophisticated rule systems but in systems that better integrate with how we actually think.

On the discovery-justification distinction, System L actually vindicates Reichenbach's framework while transforming it. The distinction between discovery procedures and verification procedures remains crucial - inductive methods can legitimately discover solutions while deductive methods verify them in deductive domains. But System L demonstrates that successful inductive inference requires integrating statistical evidence with theoretical frameworks about causation, continuity, and natural kinds. AI systems evolve away from unreliable justificatory patterns that produce Gettier-like situations toward more robust knowledge representations. This shows that the classical separation was too rigid - discovery and justification interpenetrate in ways that pure foundationalist approaches cannot accommodate.

The ampliative nature of AI-logic fundamentally challenges classical assumptions about deductive inference. When I claim that AI-logic generates new knowledge while classical logic merely rearranges existing knowledge, I'm arguing that AI-logic can model counter-entropic processes while classical logic is limited to entropic processes. AI-based interactions constitute a new epistemic faculty that generates otherwise unobtainable knowledge. This isn't mere formal transformation but genuine knowledge creation through pattern recognition integrated with theoretical understanding.

Turning to AI and epistemology, Popper's dismissal of AI hypothesis-generation as sophisticated pattern matching reveals exactly the kind of philosophical blindness I've been criticizing. The operation of modern AI systems falsifies the traditional philosophical model of induction as purely enumerative. Building AI systems that successfully replicate scientific reasoning constitutes reverse-engineering the cognitive processes underlying scientific discovery. This isn't just pattern matching - it's demonstrating that purely empiricist and purely rationalist epistemological accounts both fail.
