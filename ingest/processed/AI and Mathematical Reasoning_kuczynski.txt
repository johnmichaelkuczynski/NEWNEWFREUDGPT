From Organization to Generation: Rethinking Formalization in Light of AI

The development of artificial intelligence presents us with an unprecedented opportunity: the chance to formalize not just the products of discovery, but the process of discovery itself. Throughout history, mathematicians and logicians have created formal systems to organize pre-existing knowledge - Euclid's axiomatization of geometry, Dedekind's formalization of arithmetic, and the logical systems of Boole, Frege, and Russell. However, these formalizations share two significant limitations. First, they primarily systematize knowledge we already possess rather than generate new insights. Second, being recursive systems built from axioms and rules of inference, they can only make explicit what was already implicit in their starting points. Now, by studying how AI systems actually learn and make discoveries, we might be able to derive a different kind of formalization - a true logic of discovery that captures the generative processes of knowledge acquisition.

 The Nature and Limitations of Traditional Formalization

Traditional formalizations are fundamentally recursive systems, built from axioms and rules of inference. Their recursive nature reflects their primary function: organizing and making explicit knowledge that we already possess. When Euclid axiomatized geometry, he already understood geometric truths; his formalization provided a systematic way to arrange and derive this pre-existing knowledge. Similarly, when logicians formalized basic inference patterns, they weren't discovering that "Jim is tall" entails "something is tall" – they were systematizing relationships we already understood.

Moreover, formal systems can sometimes actively impede the acquisition of new knowledge by prematurely ruling out meaningful concepts or blocking potentially fruitful lines of inquiry. Consider these examples:

 The Case of Infinitesimals
When developing calculus, Newton and Leibniz worked productively with infinitesimals - quantities smaller than any positive number but greater than zero. Later formalization of calculus with epsilon-delta proofs seemed to show that infinitesimals were incoherent. However, this "proof" of their impossibility was too sweeping. In probability theory, for instance, the chance of selecting any specific real number from a continuous distribution must be greater than zero but smaller than any positive number - precisely an infinitesimal probability. Abraham Robinson's later development of non-standard analysis showed that infinitesimals could be rigorously formalized after all. The premature formalization of calculus without infinitesimals may have delayed this discovery.

 The Vector Space Example
A more subtle example comes from linear algebra. The standard formalization of vector spaces requires that they be closed under addition and scalar multiplication. This seemingly natural requirement actually obscures important mathematical structures. Consider the set of points in three-dimensional space with positive coordinates. This set has many vector-like properties and is crucial in applications from computer graphics to optimization theory, but it isn't technically a vector space because adding two vectors might take you outside the positive region. The formal definition of vector spaces, while useful for many purposes, can lead us to overlook or undervalue such "almost vector spaces" that are actually quite important.

 The Continuous Function Case
Early attempts to formalize the concept of continuous functions focused on the intuitive idea that you can draw them "without lifting your pencil." This led to the ε-δ definition, which became standard. However, this formalization obscured other meaningful types of continuity, such as functions that preserve adjacency relationships in discrete spaces or maintain connectivity in topological spaces. The early formalization of continuity in terms of ε-δ limits may have delayed the development of these broader concepts.

 The Nature of Traditional Formalization

 The Euclidean Example
Consider Euclid's Elements, perhaps the most influential formalization in mathematical history. Before Euclid, Greek mathematicians already knew that the angles of a triangle sum to 180 degrees, that the Pythagorean theorem held true, and that certain geometric constructions were possible. What Euclid provided was not these truths themselves, but rather a systematic organization showing how they could be derived from simpler principles.

Euclid's genius lay in identifying fundamental axioms (such as "a straight line can be drawn from any point to any other point") and postulates (like the parallel postulate) from which known geometric truths could be derived. This was a tremendous achievement in knowledge organization, but it's crucial to note that the basic geometric truths were discovered through observation, experimentation, and intuition long before their axiomatic derivation.

 Dedekind and Arithmetic
Similarly, when Richard Dedekind formalized the natural numbers (later simplified into what we now call the Peano axioms), he wasn't teaching anyone to count. His axioms - including the crucial principle of mathematical induction - provided a rigorous foundation for arithmetic operations that humans had been performing for millennia. His formalization showed how arithmetic truths could be derived from simple principles about succession (each number has a unique successor) and induction (if a property holds for zero and is preserved by the successor operation, it holds for all numbers).

This was a remarkable achievement in meta-mathematics, clarifying the foundations of arithmetic and enabling new insights about the nature of mathematical proof. However, it didn't fundamentally change how we discover or apply arithmetic truths. Children still learn arithmetic through counting, pattern recognition, and concrete examples - not through formal derivations from Dedekind's axioms.

 Boole and Formal Logic
George Boole's algebraization of logic provides another instructive example. People had been making valid logical inferences for millennia before Boole showed how logical relationships could be expressed algebraically. His system didn't teach people new ways to reason; rather, it provided a formal framework for representing and analyzing reasoning patterns that were already in use.

 The Discovery Process in AI Systems

In contrast to these formal systems, AI approaches mathematical discovery in ways that more closely resemble original human discovery processes. Here's a detailed look at how AI systems actually learn and discover mathematical patterns:

 Pattern Recognition and Generalization
When learning geometry, an AI system might:
- Process thousands of triangle images, building up an understanding of triangularity
- Notice invariant properties (three sides, angles summing to 180 degrees)
- Recognize patterns of relationship (angle-side-angle congruence)
- Generate hypotheses about general principles from specific cases

For example, an AI might discover the Pythagorean theorem not through formal proof, but by:
1. Observing many right triangles
2. Detecting the consistent relationship between side lengths
3. Recognizing this pattern holds across different scales and orientations
4. Formulating the general principle a² + b² = c²

 Relational Learning and Constraint Discovery
AI systems build understanding through:
- Identifying dependencies between variables
- Recognizing boundary conditions and constraints
- Building predictive models of how changes in one property affect others
- Detecting invariant relationships across transformations

For instance, in learning about circles, an AI might:
1. Discover that all points are equidistant from the center
2. Learn that circumference and diameter maintain a constant ratio
3. Recognize that inscribed angles subtending the same arc are equal
4. Build up complex relationships from these simpler patterns

 Hierarchical Understanding
AI systems develop mathematical knowledge in layers:
1. Basic Pattern Recognition
   - Direct observation of properties
   - Recognition of simple relationships
2. First-Order Generalizations
   - Formation of basic rules
   - Recognition of common patterns
3. Higher-Order Principles
   - Detection of patterns among patterns
   - Recognition of abstract principles
4. Meta-Level Understanding
   - Recognition of proof strategies
   - Understanding of why patterns hold

 The Role of Interaction and Testing
AI systems don't just passively observe; they:
- Generate hypotheses about mathematical relationships
- Test these hypotheses against new cases
- Refine understanding based on results
- Build increasingly sophisticated models

For example, in discovering properties of prime numbers, an AI might:
1. Generate test cases
2. Propose patterns (like the distribution of primes)
3. Test predictions against new numbers
4. Refine its understanding based on results

 The Emergence of New Mathematical Knowledge

This process of AI learning suggests a fundamentally different model of mathematical discovery than traditional formalization implies. Rather than deriving truths from axioms, mathematical knowledge emerges through:
1. Pattern recognition across multiple examples
2. Generation and testing of hypotheses
3. Gradual refinement of understanding
4. Building of increasingly abstract representations

This more closely resembles how mathematical knowledge historically developed - through observation, pattern recognition, and generalization, with formal axiomatization coming later as a way to organize and justify existing knowledge.

 Towards a New Logic of Discovery

Understanding how AI systems generate mathematical knowledge points toward a new kind of formalization - not of the results of mathematical thinking, but of the discovery process itself. This would involve formalizing:
1. Pattern recognition processes
2. Generalization strategies
3. Hypothesis generation methods
4. Testing and refinement procedures

Such a formalization would differ fundamentally from traditional logical systems in being:
- Non-recursive (not based on deriving consequences from axioms)
- Process-oriented (focusing on methods of discovery)
- Dynamic (incorporating feedback and refinement)
- Open-ended (capable of generating genuinely new insights)

 Conclusion

The development of AI not only provides new tools for knowledge generation but also offers insights into the nature of mathematical discovery itself. By studying how AI systems learn and generate new insights, we might develop a better understanding of mathematical thinking in general - one that recognizes the limitations of traditional formalization while pointing toward new ways of understanding and describing the processes of mathematical discovery and learning.

This understanding could have significant implications for mathematics education, suggesting that we should focus more on developing skills of pattern recognition and generalization rather than emphasizing formal proof and axiomatization as primary paths to mathematical knowledge. It also suggests new directions for mathematical research, focusing on understanding and formalizing the processes of discovery rather than just their results.
